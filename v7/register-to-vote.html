Frequently Asked Questions

To be eligible to register to vote in the United States, you generally must:

  • Be a U.S. citizen
  • Meet your state's residency requirements
  • Be 18 years old on or before Election Day
  • Not be currently serving a felony sentence (varies by state)
  • Not have been declared mentally incompetent by a court

Requirements may vary slightly by state, so check your state's specific eligibility rules.

The documents required to register to vote vary by state, but commonly include:

  • Valid state-issued ID (driver's license or state ID card)
  • Social Security Number (last 4 digits in most states)
  • Proof of residence (utility bill, bank statement, etc.)
  • Proof of citizenship for first-time registrants in some states

For online registration, you'll typically need your state ID number and the last four digits of your Social Security Number.

You can check your voter registration status in several ways:

  • Visit your state's election website
  • Use the national voter registration lookup tool at vote.org
  • Contact your local election office
  • Check your status when you renew your driver's license

You'll typically need to provide your name, date of birth, and address to verify your registration status.

Yes, you generally need to update your voter registration when you move. Here's what to do:

  • Moving within the same county: Update your address with your local election office.
  • Moving to a different county: You'll need to re-register in your new county.
  • Moving to a different state: You must register to vote in your new state, as voter registration doesn't transfer between states.

Many states allow you to update your registration online. If you don't update your registration before Election Day, you may be able to vote using a provisional ballot or at your old polling place, depending on state laws.

Voter registration deadlines vary by state:

  • Most states: 15-30 days before an election
  • Some states: Allow registration up to and including Election Day (same-day registration)
  • Texas: 30 days before an election
  • California: 15 days before an election (conditional registration available after the deadline)
  • New York: 25 days before an election

It's always best to register as early as possible to ensure your registration is processed in time. Check your state's specific deadline using our search tool above.

Yes, college students can register to vote, but you have a choice to make:

  • You can register at your school address (where you currently live)
  • You can remain registered at your permanent home address

You can only be registered and vote in one location. Factors to consider when deciding where to register include:

  • Where you feel more connected to the community and local issues
  • Whether your home state allows absentee voting without an excuse
  • Whether you'll be at school or home during Election Day

Many colleges and universities offer voter registration drives on campus to help students register.

You can still register to vote even if you don't have a permanent address. Here are some options:

  • Use the address of a shelter where you stay
  • Use a street corner or park where you typically stay
  • Use the address of a local service provider that you visit regularly

You'll also need to provide a mailing address where you can receive voting information. This could be:

  • A P.O. Box
  • A friend or relative's address
  • A homeless shelter or service provider that holds mail for clients

Contact your local election office for specific guidance, as procedures may vary by location.

Voting rights for people with felony convictions vary widely by state:

  • In Maine and Vermont: You never lose your right to vote, even while incarcerated.
  • In most states: Voting rights are restored automatically after release from prison, completion of parole/probation, or after a waiting period.
  • In some states: You may need to apply for restoration of voting rights or receive a pardon.

If you have a felony conviction and want to register to vote, check your state's specific laws or contact your local election office for guidance. You can also visit restoreyourvote.org for state-specific information.

How to Register to Vote

1

Check Your Eligibility

Confirm that you meet the basic requirements: U.S. citizenship, age 18+ by Election Day, and residency in your state. Each state has specific eligibility rules, so check your state's requirements.

2

Gather Required Documents

Have your identification ready. This typically includes your state-issued ID (driver's license or state ID card) and the last four digits of your Social Security Number. Some states may require additional documentation.

3

Choose Your Registration Method

Decide whether to register online, by mail, or in person. Use our search tool above to find the options available in your area. Online registration is usually the fastest method if available in your state.

4

Complete the Registration Form

Fill out all required fields on the voter registration application. Be sure to provide accurate information and sign the form (either electronically or physically, depending on your registration method).

5

Submit Before the Deadline

Make sure to submit your registration before your state's deadline, which is typically 15-30 days before an election. Registering early ensures you'll be able to vote in the next election.

6

Verify Your Registration

After submitting your registration, check your status after a few weeks to confirm it was processed. You should receive a voter registration card in the mail, but you can also verify online through your state's election website.
